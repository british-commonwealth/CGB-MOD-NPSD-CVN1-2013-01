Section 1 — Executive Summary

This document constitutes the Initial Procurement Dossier for the Commonwealth Nuclear Carrier Programme, produced by the Naval Procurement & Systems Directorate (NPSD) on behalf of the Commonwealth of Great Britain Ministry of Defence. The dossier sets out the identified capability requirement, proposed platform parameters, outline propulsion and aviation configuration, and indicative acquisition timeline.

The Commonwealth Nuclear Carrier (CVN) is intended to provide the Commonwealth Navy with a next-generation nuclear-powered carrier strike capability, enabling the sustained independent projection of air power at strategic range without dependence on land basing or replenishment of conventional fuel. The platform is designed to operate as the capital asset of a Commonwealth carrier strike group, embarking a full-spectrum fixed-wing air group and providing the command and support infrastructure for sustained strike, air defence, and maritime control operations across the most demanding threat environments anticipated in the planning horizon.

Programme definition work is ongoing. This document is presented to the Investment Approvals Board as the basis for approval to proceed to the Concept Definition phase. All values are subject to revision as programme definition matures and nuclear regulatory engagement with NRSA advances.

Section 2 — Strategic Requirement

2.1 Identified Deficiencies in the Current Carrier Force

The Commonwealth Navy's current carrier capability is provided by conventionally powered platforms of limited displacement and aviation capacity. Analysis conducted by the Naval Staff Directorate and DEC(CV) has identified the following deficiencies which the CVN programme is intended to address:

Endurance and Operational Reach: Conventionally powered carriers require regular refuelling, constraining operational tempo and introducing logistic dependency that is exploitable by adversaries. A nuclear-powered platform eliminates aviation-fuel-driven range limitations and removes propulsion fuel from the logistics burden entirely, releasing storage volume for aviation fuel and ordnance. The operational radius achievable at sustained high speed by a nuclear-powered platform significantly exceeds that of any conventionally powered equivalent.

Aviation Capacity: Current platforms are constrained in both hangar volume and flight deck area. The projected threat environment requires a significantly larger organic air group to sustain area air defence, strike, and maritime patrol concurrently. The CVN is designed to embark a minimum of 85 aircraft in normal operational configuration, with surge capacity in excess of 100.

Power Generation: Next-generation aircraft launch systems, directed energy weapons, advanced sensor arrays, and the emerging requirement to power embarked directed energy close-in defence systems exceed the generating capacity of conventionally powered hulls of acceptable displacement. Nuclear propulsion provides surplus electrical generation capacity as an inherent characteristic of the plant, without the displacement and fuel penalties associated with additional diesel generation.

2.2 Strategic Policy Basis

The CVN programme is assessed as the single most significant capital platform investment available to the Commonwealth in terms of strategic effect. The platform's ability to project credible fixed-wing air power independently of host-nation basing or permission is assessed as essential to the Commonwealth's strategic autonomy and alliance leadership role across the planning horizon.

Section 3 — Platform Overview

The Commonwealth CVN is a new-design nuclear-powered aircraft carrier of approximately 95,000 tonnes full-load displacement, configured around a four-catapult, four-arresting-wire conventional take-off and landing (CTOL) flight deck arrangement. The design incorporates an angled landing deck, island superstructure to starboard, four deck-edge aircraft elevators, and two nuclear reactor compartments providing propulsion and ship's power generation. The hangar deck is a full-length, full-width arrangement capable of accommodating the planned air group with adequate maintenance and storage margins.

The CVN design is platform-for-life, with the hull sized and electrically provisioned to support successive capability increments over a service life of not less than fifty years. The nuclear plant is to be designed to support two full refuelling cycles during this service life, with mid-life hull modernisation the primary vehicle for capability insertion.

Section 5 — Nuclear Propulsion Plant

Restricted — Nuclear Sensitive: The parameters in this section are subject to additional access restrictions under NPSD/NRSA/001. All design data relating to the nuclear plant is to be handled in accordance with the enhanced protective marking NUC-SENS in addition to the OS-NPSD marking of this document. 5.1 Reactor Configuration

The CVN is to be fitted with two pressurised water reactor (PWR) units, providing propulsion power to four shaft lines via steam turbines and reduction gearing, and generating electrical power for the ship's systems, aircraft launch equipment, and combat systems. The reactor plant is to be designed and qualified in accordance with Commonwealth nuclear safety regulations under the authority of the Nuclear Regulatory and Safety Authority (NRSA).

Nuclear Plant — Outline Parameters
Reactor TypePressurised Water Reactor (PWR); 2 units; specific design subject to nuclear regulatory engagement and contractor competition
Propulsion ConfigurationSteam turbine drive to 4 shafts; total installed shaft power not less than 260,000 shp
Propulsors4 × fixed-pitch propellers; estimated diameter 6.4 m per propeller
Rudders4 × balanced rudders (twin per pair of shafts)
Electrical GenerationNot less than 200 MW total installed electrical capacity; margin reserved for EMALS, EAADS, directed energy systems, and expanded sensor suites
Core LifeSubject to NRSA engagement; minimum design objective is a 25-year core life before first refuelling; specific enrichment and core loading classified
Reactor CompartmentTwo separate compartments; forward and aft of midships; each housing one PWR unit; shielding, containment, and access arrangements in accordance with NRSA-approved nuclear safety case
Emergency PropulsionEmergency diesel generators and auxiliary propulsion provisions to be incorporated; detailed configuration subject to safety case

5.2 Nuclear Safety and Regulatory Framework

The design, construction, and operation of the nuclear plant is subject to the full requirements of the CGB-MOD Nuclear Safety Management framework and NRSA regulatory oversight. The programme shall not proceed to Main Gate without a preliminary nuclear safety case accepted by NRSA, and construction shall not commence without a design nuclear safety case accepted in accordance with NRSA authorisation procedures.

  • Nuclear safety case to be prepared and maintained by the nuclear plant design authority throughout all programme phases
  • Independent nuclear safety assessment to be conducted by NRSA at each programme gate
  • Crew nuclear qualification and training pipeline to be established not less than 4 years prior to the target IOC date
  • Port access and basing arrangements are subject to separate environmental and nuclear regulatory consenting processes not within scope of this document
Note on Electrical Generation Margin: The total installed electrical generating capacity is to provide a minimum 30% surplus above the projected propulsion, hotel, and EMALS operational load at maximum sortie generation rate. This margin is expressly reserved to support integration of directed energy weapon systems, next-generation radar installations, and future unmanned carrier aviation systems. No reduction to this margin shall be accepted during design development without formal approval of DEC(CV) and NPSD.

Section 6 — Aviation Systems & Launch Equipment

6.1 Flight Deck Arrangement

The CVN flight deck is configured as a conventional take-off and arrested landing (CTOL) arrangement, with an angled landing deck of not less than 9° inclination relative to the ship's centreline. This arrangement provides simultaneous launch and recovery capability and is the configuration necessary to exploit the full range of fixed-wing carrier aviation types, including future heavier and faster aircraft generations that may be constrained by ski-jump or short take-off configurations.

Flight Deck AreaNot less than 18,000 m²
Angled Deck9° inclination; full-length landing area; compatible with simultaneous bow catapult launch operations
Aircraft Deck ParkProvision for not less than 30 aircraft in deck park configuration in addition to hangar stowage
Deck-Edge Elevators4 × deck-edge aircraft elevators; minimum capacity 36,000 kg per elevator; positions at bow port, waist port, waist starboard, and stern starboard
Elevator Cycle TimeNot to exceed 60 seconds for a fully laden aircraft transfer between hangar and flight deck

6.2 Electromagnetic Aircraft Launch System (EMALS)

The CVN is to be fitted with an Electromagnetic Aircraft Launch System in place of conventional steam catapults. EMALS provides a controlled, programmable launch energy profile, reducing airframe fatigue loads relative to steam catapult launch, while offering substantially greater flexibility in the range of aircraft weights and types that can be launched. Four EMALS tracks are to be provided.

Number of EMALS Tracks4 (2 bow tracks; 2 angled-deck waist tracks)
Launch Energy RangeScalable; compatible with aircraft of not less than 4,500 kg and not more than 45,000 kg launch weight
Sortie Generation RateNot less than 120 sorties per day sustained; surge rate not less than 160 sorties per day for periods of up to 12 hours
Power SourceDedicated energy storage system charged from ship's nuclear-generated electrical supply; specific energy storage technology subject to system design
CompatibilityAll current and planned Commonwealth and allied carrier aviation types; provision for future unmanned carrier-launched vehicles of up to 20,000 kg

6.3 Arresting Gear

The CVN is to be fitted with an Advanced Arresting Gear (AAG) system, providing a controlled recovery force profile analogous to the controlled launch profile of EMALS. The system eliminates the hydraulic arresting gear fluid systems of conventional installations, reducing maintenance burden and providing compatibility with the full range of planned air group types.

Arresting Wires3 wires; cross-deck pendant arrangement; wire spacing to CTOL carrier standard
Recovery Weight RangeCompatible with aircraft up to 45,000 kg recovery weight at the full range of approach speeds
Cycle TimeWire reset time not to exceed 45 seconds between arrested recoveries

6.4 Air Wing

The CVN is designed to embark and sustain the following air wing composition in normal operational configuration. The composition reflects the full spectrum of carrier strike group missions including strike, fleet air defence, maritime patrol, airborne early warning, and anti-submarine warfare.

Section 7 — Combat Systems & Self-Defence

The CVN is not designed as an offensive surface combatant in its own right; its primary combat power is delivered through the embarked air wing and the coordinated operations of its associated strike group. The ship's organic self-defence systems are designed to provide a layered close-in and short-range defence capability sufficient to defeat threats that penetrate the outer layers of the strike group's air defence envelope. The design philosophy prioritises aviation operations and does not permit self-defence systems to constrain flight deck area or sortie generation rate.

7.1 Vertical Launch System

Total VLS Cells64 (self-defence missile types only; no land-attack or anti-ship missile carriage)
Primary Missile TypeShort to medium-range area self-defence interceptors; specific type subject to selection
SitingDistributed across sponsons to avoid interference with flight operations; fore and aft batteries

7.2 Close-In Weapon Systems

Gun CIWS Mounts4 × 20 mm / 30 mm Automated Gun CIWS (distributed; full-coverage arcs)

Directed Energy (Future)Power, cooling, and structural provisions incorporated at baseline for future high-energy laser close-in defence systems; no system commitment at this stage
Decoy SystemsMultiple chaff and infrared decoy launcher positions; active offboard decoy provision

Section 8 — Sensors & Electronic Warfare

8.1 Primary Radar System

The CVN is to be fitted with a next-generation multi-function AESA radar system, providing the primary air search, tracking, and fire control function for the ship's self-defence systems and the recognised air picture contribution to the strike group. The radar system must be capable of supporting the coordination of the air wing during high-tempo launch and recovery operations alongside the fleet air defence mission.

Radar TypeMulti-function AESA; dual or quad-face fixed array; integrated with ship's combat management system
Simultaneous Track CapacityNot less than 2,000 tracks (combined air and surface)
Target CategoriesFixed and rotary wing; sea-skimming and ballistic anti-ship missiles; UAV formations; low-observable platforms; surface vessels

8.2 Secondary and Navigation Radars

Air Traffic Control RadarDedicated ATC radar system for management of embarked and transiting air traffic; independent of primary combat radar
Navigation RadarsMultiple; compliant with COLREGS and naval navigation standards; integrated bridge management system
Precision Approach RadarCarrier-controlled approach radar system; supports all-weather aircraft recovery

8.3 Electronic Warfare Suite

Radar Warning Receivers (RWR)Full-sphere; library-based identification; integrated with combat management threat assessment and decoy management
Electronic Support Measures (ESM)Wide-band passive intercept and analysis; cueing for EW, weapons, and air wing mission planning
Electronic Countermeasures (ECM)Active jamming; coordinated with embarked electronic attack air wing for cooperative EW operations

Section 9 — Unmanned Systems Integration

9.1 Unmanned Carrier-Launched Aircraft

The CVN is to be designed from the outset as a carrier capable of operating unmanned carrier-launched aircraft (UCLA) alongside crewed types, with no modification to the hull or primary structure. The EMALS launch system, AAG recovery system, deck-edge elevators, and hangar configuration shall be selected and sized to be compatible with current planned UCLA types and with projected future types anticipated within the platform's service life.

UCLA Category (IOC)ISR; strike support; tanker; future armed variants to be assessed as capability matures
UCLA Integration (IOC)Minimum 6 UCLA aircraft in nominal IOC air wing; growth to 15 in increment 2
Control ArchitectureIntegrated with combat management system; supervised autonomous operation; human-in-the-loop weapons release requirement at IOC
Data LinkReal-time sensor feed; mission re-tasking from ship's operations room and from air wing mission control station

9.2 Unmanned Surface and Undersea Vehicles

An enlarged mission bay is incorporated in the stern of the hull to provide flexibility for launch and recovery of unmanned surface and undersea vehicles supporting the strike group's extended maritime surveillance and mine countermeasures requirements.

Mission Bay LocationStern; accessible via stern ramp and side ports; isolated from aviation fuel and ordnance magazines
Modular CapabilityUSV launch and recovery; UUV maintenance and deployment; special operations craft; containerised mission systems (ISO 20-foot footprint)
Mission Bay VolumeNot less than 2,000 m³ usable volume

Section 10 — Digital & Network Architecture

10.1 Combat Management System

The CVN is designed around an integrated combat management system (CMS) which provides the central processing, display, and data management function for all sensor, weapon, aviation, and communications systems aboard the platform. The CMS is conceived as the primary node of the carrier strike group's networked command architecture, supporting coordinated multi-domain operations across all attached surface, subsurface, and aviation elements.

  • Full sensor fusion: real-time integration of ship's radars, ESM, RWR, air wing data links, and off-board sensor inputs into a unified recognised joint picture
  • Strike group coordination: CMS to serve as the primary command and control node for the carrier strike group; real-time picture sharing with all attached units
  • Air traffic management integration: CMS to interface directly with ship's ATC radar and UCLA control architecture; all flight deck operations to be visible within the operations room
  • Cooperative engagement capability: full integration with Commonwealth and allied cooperative engagement networks; exploitation of remote fire control data from strike group surface and airborne assets
  • Secure multi-domain data links: not less than five simultaneous secure tactical data links; compliant with current and anticipated alliance standards
  • Open systems architecture: conforming to open standards for through-life capability updates and reduced single-source dependency

Section 11 — Capability Roadmap

The following programme phases are proposed for the Commonwealth Nuclear Carrier Programme. The programme is treated as having commenced in 2011 under the Capital Ship Renewal workstream, with an accelerated intended sea-trials target of 2018 and final fleet delivery by 2020. Given the complexity of nuclear propulsion qualification, the scale of the platform, and the novelty of EMALS and AAG integration, the compressed programme timeline is assessed as high risk and is subject to revision following nuclear regulatory engagement, Main Gate contractor selection, and yard-capacity review. All phase transitions are subject to investment approval at the appropriate decision gate in accordance with the CGB-MOD Acquisition Management System.

Concept Definition		 2011 — 2013 Finalisation of URD and SRD; nuclear plant concept selection and preliminary NRSA engagement; EMALS and AAG technology assessment; contractor pre-qualification and market engagement; hull form concept studies; alliance liaison with US Navy on nuclear carrier design experience. Initial Gate (IG) — Investment Approvals Board. Treasury approval for concept definition expenditure.

Full Business Case development for Main Gate. Main Gate (MG) — Secretary of State approval. Full Business Case to IAB and Treasury. NRSA design safety case acceptance mandatory precondition.

NRSA operational nuclear safety case acceptance required before fleet delivery.
Phase 5
IOC & FOC		 Target: 2020 Commissioning, IOC declaration, air wing workup, strike group integration, and crew/air wing training pipeline completion. Final fleet delivery and FOC targeted for 2020 following completion of full air wing integration, UCLA initial operating capability, and declaration of strike group readiness. Future increments: directed energy CIWS, increment 2 UCLA, mid-life modernisation. IOC — DCNS. FOC — First Sea Lord. Future increment approvals subject to individual programme authorities.
Programme Risk Note: The CVN programme is assessed as the highest-complexity surface ship programme attempted by the Commonwealth Navy. Risk is concentrated in nuclear plant qualification and NRSA engagement timelines, EMALS and AAG integration, and the scale and novelty of the industrial task. The programme timeline is to be reviewed at each decision gate with reference to nuclear regulatory progress, contractor performance, and alliance experience data. A compressed contingency reserve is held against the 2018 sea-trials target, with fleet delivery protected as the primary 2020 milestone.
